Starting

Why starting is often the hardest part

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

There are days when the task itself might actually be manageable, but the beginning feels huge. You know roughly what needs doing. You may even have time. Still, the move from not-doing to doing can feel thick and heavy, as if there is too much drag between you and the first step.

That kind of stuckness is hard to explain because it looks irrational from the outside. Once you are in, you may work quite well. But getting in costs far more than other people see. They notice the minutes of action, not the invisible effort it took to cross the threshold.

Starting is not one tiny movement

A lot of advice treats starting like a simple switch. Just begin. Just do five minutes. Just open the document. In reality, starting often contains many smaller demands: leaving the previous thing, choosing where to enter, tolerating uncertainty, managing resistance and staying with the task past the first bit of friction.

For neurodivergent systems, transitions themselves can be tiring. They ask your brain to move channels. If you are already low on energy, overstimulated or half-hooked into something else, that transition can feel bigger than the work waiting on the other side.

Sometimes the first step is not actually clear

A task can sound simple while still hiding a lot of decisions. "Reply to that email" may really mean: remember the context, decide on your tone, work out what matters, figure out how much to say and prepare for the reaction. No wonder the body does not leap forward.

The same thing happens with chores, admin and creative work. The blockage is often not the title of the task but the pile of smaller choices underneath it. When those choices stay invisible, it looks like you are the problem. Really, your brain is reacting to complexity it has already sensed.

The trap of making the start too big

Another reason starting feels heavy is that many of us secretly define it as a full commitment. If I start, I should keep going. If I open it, I should finish it. If I begin, I should be productive now. That makes the first step feel less like an opening and more like a contract you are not sure you can keep.

Gentler starts work better. Not: now I must get this done. More like: now I will make the doorway smaller. Open the file. Write the first line. Put the supplies on the table. Name the first move out loud. Small starts matter because they reduce the cost of crossing from stillness into motion.

What helps when beginning keeps feeling sticky

It helps to plan beginnings, not just tasks. What exactly counts as the opening move? What can already be decided? What can be laid out, shortened or simplified in advance? Some days a timer helps. Some days a hard stop helps more. Some days the most honest move is checking whether your system has room for this transition at all.

That is often where PRISMA Day helps me. Not because it magically fixes starting, but because it makes the entry point clearer. You check energy, needs and work style first. Then the first step can be chosen in the context of the day you are actually in, instead of the day you wish you were having.

Beginning does not have to look impressive

You do not need a beautiful start. You do not need to feel fully ready. You do not need to know that you will stay with the task for an hour. A clumsy start still counts as a start. Often that is enough to make the day feel less sealed shut.

If starting is often the hardest part for you, that does not mean you are lazy or incapable of structure. It usually means the doorway has been made too narrow, too abstract or too forceful. A better system widens it.
