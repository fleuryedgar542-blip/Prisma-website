How to notice sensory overload before it gets too much

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 6 min read

← Back to blog

Sensory overload does not always arrive like a crash. Often it starts quietly. Sound feels sharper. Your body tenses faster. A simple question annoys you more than usual. You may still think you can push through. That is often the point where things begin to tip.

Early signs are often subtle

  • You startle more easily.
  • It gets harder to find words.
  • You become shorter, quieter or more reactive.
  • Small decisions suddenly feel expensive.
  • Your body feels hot, tight or restless.

Those signs may look small, but they are usually the moment when a short pause, less input, food, silence or distance can still change the direction of the day.

Why it is hard to notice in time

Many people are used to ignoring signals until they become impossible to ignore. That makes sense if you have spent years pushing through. But it also means you often step in too late.

A practical question that helps

Instead of asking, “Am I okay?” ask: “What costs me more effort now than it did an hour ago?” Sensory overload is usually cumulative. The change matters more than any single dramatic sign.
