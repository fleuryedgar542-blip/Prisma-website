Why small tasks can feel impossible

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

Replying to a message. Filling in a form. Putting a glass in the kitchen. Rescheduling an appointment you have been avoiding for days. On paper, these are small tasks. That is exactly why they can create so much shame. If the task is so small, why is it still not done?

What is easy to miss is that small on paper does not always mean small for your nervous system. A task can hold uncertainty, decisions, transitions, social tension or a chain of hidden mini-steps. When all of that sits underneath one simple task label, it makes sense that the body does not experience it as small at all.

Small on the outside can still feel heavy on the inside

Tasks are usually named by their visible action. "Send the email." "Do the laundry." "Make the call." But under those labels there may be many other things: remembering the context, deciding on wording, tolerating the reply, gathering supplies, choosing a moment, or leaving what you are doing now.

For ADHD and autistic people, that hidden layer matters. Your brain often senses the full stack before you consciously name it. So while other people only see one small action, your system is already reacting to multiple bits of friction at once.

Emotional weight makes tasks much bigger

Some tasks are small but emotionally loaded. Opening a bill can trigger dread. Replying to a message can involve guilt. Sending an email can stir up fear of misunderstanding or conflict. At that point the task is no longer just practical. It is carrying an emotional charge as well.

That can make the body freeze, avoid or delay, even when the actual action would only take a few minutes. The problem is not laziness. The system is often trying to protect you from something it reads as stressful, even if the task looks ordinary from the outside.

Transitions are part of the problem too

Small tasks often sit in between other things. They are the things you are supposed to do "quickly" or "while you are at it." But switching attention costs energy. If you are in deep focus, tired, overstimulated or already carrying too many loose threads, that transition can feel more expensive than the task itself.

That is one reason tiny jobs sometimes stay stuck for hours or days. You are not only doing the task. You are paying the cost of entering it, leaving something else and then trying to re-enter whatever came before.

What helps more than forcing yourself

It helps to stop calling the task small and start getting specific. What exactly makes it sticky? Is it social tension? Decision fatigue? A hidden sequence of steps? A difficult beginning? Once the problem becomes more precise, it often feels less mysterious and less total.

Sometimes you can split the task. Sometimes you can do the setup without doing the whole thing. Sometimes you can move it to a better moment. And sometimes the most useful thing is simply admitting that your system does not experience this as small today. That honesty often reduces the fight.
