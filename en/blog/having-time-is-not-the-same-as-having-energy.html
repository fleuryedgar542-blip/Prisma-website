Having time is not the same as having energy

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

An empty afternoon can look like freedom on paper. In your body it may not feel like that at all. You may have hours. No meetings. No urgent deadlines. And still the thing you hoped would finally fit does not happen. That gap can feel incredibly frustrating because from the outside it looks so obvious.

We are used to treating time as the main problem. If something does not get done, you must not have had enough hours. But on many difficult days, time is not the real limit. Energy is. And those two line up far less neatly than people think.

An open calendar is not the same as an open system

You can have hours available and still not feel any room. Because your head is crowded. Because your body is tired. Because you have already used too much switching energy. Because your nervous system hit its edge long before the empty afternoon arrived. In that case, time does not feel relieving. It simply exposes that something else is missing.

That is also why free days can disappoint people. You hope everything will finally happen then. But if your system mostly needs recovery, extra time feels less like opportunity and more like pressure. There is suddenly more space you are supposed to make use of. If you cannot, it starts to feel like waste.

Why this gets explained badly

From the outside, people often answer this by saying you need to plan better. Or become more disciplined. But that advice confuses capacity with scheduling. If your energy is low, an open plan does not automatically solve anything. Then the problem is not a lack of time. It is a lack of carry capacity.

That distinction matters, because otherwise you keep treating yourself like you should function well as long as the calendar looks tidy enough. Meanwhile your body has been telling a different story for hours.

You also hear it in the way people talk about "finally having time." As if time automatically becomes usable the moment it opens up. But free time without recovery, without quiet or without mental room often does not feel free at all. It just becomes another empty space that is supposed to contain performance.

Energy leaks in more places than work

Many people only measure energy against visible tasks. But energy also goes to noise, expectation, social recovery, decision-making, travel, poor sleep and all the invisible self-management that difficult days ask from you. That means you can have done very little "on paper" and still be completely used up.

If you do not include that, it remains confusing why an empty afternoon does not help. It looks like you are failing to use the time. In reality, you may already be compensating for ten things that never appear in the calendar.

The small shift is asking a different question

What helps is a different first question. Do I have room for this today? That sounds small, but it changes a lot. Room is not only about minutes. It is also about stimulation, recovery, friction and how many transitions your system can still tolerate.

Some days you may have time for three things but room for one. Other days you have very little time but enough energy to do something clearly. Once you learn to see that distinction, your week often becomes less confusing.

Why PRISMA fits this better than a normal planner

Normal planners usually treat time as the main variable. They are built around blocks, hours and arrangement. That misses a lot when your capacity changes constantly. That is why I reach for PRISMA Day on days when I technically have time but cannot feel a true start. Energy first. Need first. Then maybe something fits.

And if the issue is not just today but the repeated habit of overestimating your whole week because it looks like there is "enough time," then PRISMA Week helps more. Not because it plans harder, but because it helps translate several days into something your system can actually carry.

The mini-solution is not filling more time

Many people on hard days do not need more structure so much as truer measures. An afternoon does not need to be fully used to count. Rest counts. Recovery counts. A day can "fail" on paper and still make perfect sense in the body.

That shift often takes some shame out of it. Not all of it. But enough to stop fighting a wall that was never really about hours in the first place.

And that often changes the rest of the week too. When you stop treating every empty hour as evidence of what you should have done, it becomes a little easier to feel more realistically what tomorrow or later might hold.
