The myth of the perfect routine

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

There is something deeply appealing about the idea of the perfect routine. If you could just find the right morning flow, the right sequence, the right set of habits, maybe everything would settle down. Less chaos. Less procrastination. Less second-guessing. Especially when your brain gets overwhelmed easily, a perfect routine can sound almost like rescue.

But in real life, that perfect routine often becomes a very narrow structure you have to balance on every day. One poor night of sleep, one unexpected appointment, one overstimulating morning, and the whole thing feels broken. Then it looks like you failed, when the truth is usually much simpler: the system was too rigid for an actual life.

Why perfect routines sound so comforting

Routines promise predictability, and predictability can be a real need. That is not the problem. ADHD, autism, burnout and overload can all make steadiness feel precious. The trouble starts when structure only seems valid if it happens in exactly the same way every single day.

At that point, routine quietly stops being support and starts becoming measurement. You are no longer using a rhythm. You are judging yourself against one. The tighter that standard becomes, the easier it is to feel like the weak link whenever your day does not match the script.

Most routines do not fail because you are undisciplined

They usually fail because they leave too little room for variation. Energy changes. Sleep shifts. Sensory load builds. The week does not move in a straight line. If a routine cannot flex, then every ordinary disruption becomes a breaking point. You do not need much friction before the whole system starts to feel unusable.

That hits many neurodivergent people especially hard. Not because they are incapable of routine, but because highly structured routines often ignore capacity, transition time and recovery. The plan works on your neatest days, and collapses on the days you actually need it most.

What often works better

A looser rhythm is often more durable than a perfect routine. Instead of writing a full behavioural script for yourself, it can help to think in anchor points. Not: every morning I do these seven exact things in this exact order. More: I want a soft beginning, a midday check-in and some kind of clear closing moment at the end of the day.

That difference matters. A rigid routine asks for repetition of behaviour. An anchor asks for repetition of intention. You stay connected to what you need, without demanding the same execution every time. That makes structure far kinder on days when your brain or body is simply not operating at full range.

How to notice when structure has become pressure

One useful question is whether your routine is carrying you, or whether you are carrying it. If it mainly creates tension, keeps needing rescue and activates shame every time you miss a step, it may need to become simpler and softer. That does not mean abandoning structure. It means building one that can survive ordinary life.

Where does your energy go? What kinds of rhythms feel steadier? What keeps looking good on paper but not in practice? That is a gentler basis for structure.

Good routines are often unremarkable

The most useful routines are rarely impressive. They usually feel quiet, sustainable and a little boring. That is not a flaw. It is often a sign that the structure is not demanding too much from you in order to exist.

The perfect routine probably is a myth. A routine that bends without breaking is much more realistic, and much more helpful.
