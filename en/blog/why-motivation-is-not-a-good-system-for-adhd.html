Why motivation is not a good system for ADHD

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

A lot of people grow up with the idea that motivation comes first. First you need to feel ready. First you need enough drive. Then the action will follow. With ADHD, that idea can become painful because motivation is often very real, but not very stable. It can be strong one night and almost unreachable the next morning.

That makes people doubt themselves. You know you can care. You know you can want something. You may even have bursts of intense focus and energy. But wanting something is not the same as having reliable access to starting, deciding and following through. When those things change from day to day, motivation becomes a shaky foundation.

Motivation is weather, not flooring

Motivation is useful when it shows up, but it is too changeable to build your whole system on. Some days it arrives because a task feels interesting, urgent, new or emotionally alive. Other days the exact same task feels dead on contact. If your planning only works when your brain happens to light up on cue, then it is not really a system. It is more like waiting for good weather.

That is one reason standard advice often falls flat. A lot of productivity language assumes that if something matters to you, you should be able to do it consistently just by deciding to. ADHD does not work that neatly. Interest, context, clarity, friction and nervous-system state matter much more than many people realise.

Why this gets misread so often

From the outside, the pattern can look inconsistent or careless. One day you can work for hours. Another day a simple task feels impossible to enter. People then reach for the usual explanations: not enough discipline, not enough commitment, too much distraction, too little effort. But a lot of the time the issue is not whether you care. It is whether your brain has access to the task.

Access matters more than intention. Can you see the first step? Can your body tolerate starting? Is the task small enough to feel real instead of abstract? Is there enough steadiness in your system to stay with it for even ten minutes? When the answer is no, more pressure about motivation usually does not help. It just adds shame on top of friction.

What tends to help more

It helps to stop asking whether you are motivated enough and start asking what would make the task easier to enter. Maybe the first step needs to be smaller. Maybe the choice needs to be made the night before. Maybe the materials need to be visible. Maybe the job for today is not to finish, but to lower the threshold for tomorrow.

That sounds simple, but it changes the whole tone. You stop treating motivation like a moral test. Instead, you build structure that still works when your energy is average, your attention is scattered or your system feels slow. You make it more likely that action can begin before motivation fully arrives, which is often exactly how motivation appears in the first place.

Structure does not have to mean forcing yourself

A lot of ADHD-friendly structure is not about pushing harder. It is about reducing drag. Fewer decisions. Gentler starts. Clearer boundaries. A task you can actually picture. A plan that assumes your day is real, not ideal. Good structure does not replace self-awareness. It gives self-awareness somewhere to land.

That is why I built PRISMA Day the way I did. Not as a tool for your most productive days, but as something you can still use when motivation is missing. You begin with energy, needs and work style. Only then do you choose what fits. That makes motivation welcome when it appears, without making it the only door into action.

What to keep with you

If motivation in your life is inconsistent, that does not mean you are unreliable. It means you probably need a calmer entry point than mainstream advice gives you. A system that helps on ordinary days is usually worth more than a plan that only works when you are fully switched on.

You do not have to wait until you feel like doing everything. Often you just need a smaller door into the first thing.
