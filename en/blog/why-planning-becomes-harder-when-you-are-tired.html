Tiredness

Why planning becomes harder when you are tired

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

Some days you know what needs to happen, but turning that into a plan feels strangely hard. Not because you do not care. Not because you do not want structure. Your mind just feels thicker, slower or more crowded than usual. Every choice seems to weigh more.

That often happens when you are tired. With ADHD or autism, it can become very obvious because planning is not only practical. It is also cognitive work. And on tired days, that thinking work can cost more than you have available.

Planning is often treated as if it were neutral. You write things down, put them in order and begin. But when you are tired, it rarely feels that simple. You are not only deciding what matters. You are also switching, weighing, returning to the task, holding back doubt and sometimes trying to think through fog. That is a lot.

Tiredness does not only slow down your body

It also changes the amount of mental room you have. You often notice it in small ways. You read a list and forget what you just saw. You open your calendar and feel pressure before you feel clarity. You think about three tasks at once and suddenly none of them seem manageable.

That does not mean you are bad at planning. It means planning depends on functions that become harder to access when you are tired: dividing attention, choosing priorities, estimating time, slowing impulsive reactions and moving from thinking into action.

When that foundation feels less stable, planning can become something you have to get through before it starts helping. That is why even a small plan can feel too big.

Why tiredness also makes planning emotionally heavier

There is often another layer underneath it. Many people plan from the image of who they were yesterday, last month or in a better period. You make a plan for a version of yourself who is not fully available today.

You feel that gap straight away. You can see what should be possible, but your body or mind is not lining up with it. That is where shame often enters. You may think you need to push harder, while the real problem is that your plan is ignoring tiredness.

That is also why tired days can turn into avoidance so quickly. Not always because you want to do nothing, but because everything starts carrying too much meaning. Every choice feels final. Every task feels like proof that you are behind. Every plan feels like something you will fail later.

Planning takes decisions, and decisions take energy

An ordinary plan contains dozens of tiny choices. Do I do this first or later. Is this important or can it wait. How big is this task really. Where do I start. What if it takes longer. What if I get stuck halfway through.

On a clearer day, many of those decisions happen almost automatically. On a tired day, you have to lift each one on purpose. That is what makes planning feel sticky. The tasks may not be different, but the road toward them is less automatic.

That is also why some people avoid planning altogether when they are tired. It may look irrational from the outside, because that is exactly when direction would help. But it makes sense. If the planning itself is already too demanding, doing no planning at all can feel like the least heavy option.

What usually does not help

What often backfires is making the plan bigger in the hope that more structure will create more control. A strict schedule, a long task list or a beautifully organised day can look reassuring, but it can also push your system further into resistance.

Harsher self-talk usually does not help either. Sentences like “I just need to get on with it” may create a brief push, but they often make the threshold feel harder. Tiredness does not disappear because you judge it. You just end up carrying more tension on top of it.

What helps when planning feels heavy

What tends to work better is making planning smaller than your first instinct. Not smaller because you are lowering the bar forever, but smaller because your brain still needs to be able to hold it. Sometimes it helps not to plan the whole day, but only the next block. Or only the next step.

For day planning, I use PRISMA Day most on days like that. Not because it solves tiredness, but because it starts with your energy instead of an ideal version of you. That changes the tone immediately. You do not have to pretend you have more room than you do.

It also helps when a plan does not have to be complete. One task, one rest point and one thing that genuinely does not need to happen can be far more useful on a tired day than a polished schedule full of good intentions.

If tiredness stretches across several days, it can help to zoom out a little. PRISMA Week works better for that, because it helps you see patterns you usually miss inside single days. You may notice that the same kinds of days keep draining you, or that planning falls apart after specific kinds of overload.

Softer planning is still real planning

I think that matters to say clearly. A gentler plan is not weak. A small plan is not a failed one. And a day where your main goal is to stop things from slipping further is still a day where you are steering.

Many neurodivergent people have learned to think of planning as something tight, productive and visible. But sometimes planning is simply the practice of not loading your day more heavily than your system can carry. That is not less serious. It is often more honest.

Maybe the problem is not you, but the kind of planning

If planning becomes harder when you are tired, that does not automatically mean you are bad at planning. It may mean that most systems you tried were built for days with steadier energy, more spare attention and more flexibility than you actually have.

A useful system makes room for fluctuation. It does not expect you to begin every morning from the same place. It allows gentleness without becoming vague. And it helps you choose without making every choice feel like walking through mud.

That, for me, is the difference between a plan that only looks good on paper and a plan that actually supports you. Tired days reveal that difference very quickly.
