Stuckness

I want to do it but I still do nothing

Written by Fleur Yedgar · March 2026 · 8 min read

← Back to blog

Some days the strange part is not that you do not care. The strange part is that you do care. You really do want to start. You know the thing matters. You may think about it all day. And still nothing happens. You scroll. You pace. You open something and close it again. By the end of the day it feels like you got in your own way from start to finish.

That kind of stuckness creates a specific kind of shame. If you did not want to do it, it would make more sense. But you do want to. So the silence between wanting and doing starts to feel personal. Like a character flaw. Like proof that you are unreliable.

Wanting is not the same as being able to start

A lot of advice quietly treats wanting and doing as almost the same thing. If you are motivated, you will move. If you care enough, you will act. But with overstimulation, low energy, ADHD, autism or stress, that link often breaks. You can genuinely want something and still have no clean path into the first step.

That broken link is often where the real problem sits. Not in desire. Not in values. In the movement between them. Inside, you feel pressure. Outside, people see stillness. That gap gets misread as laziness all the time.

Why it feels so irrational

It also looks irrational from the inside. You may think, if I have been mentally circling this for hours, why can I not just do it? But thinking about something is not the same as having access to action. Sometimes all that internal effort leaves even less room for the first move.

You think about the open ends, the reactions, the effort, the chain of things that comes after, the energy you may not have. So you are not passive at all. Your system is often very active. Just not in a way that produces visible work.

Why regular planning only partly helps

When you are in that state, a normal planner can add pressure instead of support. You see everything again. You feel behind again. You have to choose a starting point. You have to rank things. You have to organise yourself at the exact moment organising is what is failing.

That is why planning advice often misses the point on hard days. It asks for more mental work when the problem is already too much mental work at the doorway.

The shift is realising you are not blocked on care

Things got lighter for me when I stopped explaining every stuck day through motivation. The question became less, do I want this enough? And more, where is the start getting jammed? Sometimes it was overstimulation. Sometimes too much uncertainty. Sometimes pressure. Sometimes a system that was already spent.

That shift matters because it changes the kind of help you look for. If you think the issue is desire, you will mostly reach for discipline. If you see that the start is blocked somewhere else, you can build a gentler entry instead.

What helps more on days like this

What often helps is not talking yourself into it harder. It is reducing the size of the threshold. Not the whole thing. The entrance. Often you are not stuck on the full project. You are stuck on opening it, switching into it, tolerating the first friction, or carrying the expectation attached to it.

That is where PRISMA Day helps me. It shows me what today actually is. If my energy is low and my head is full, then I stop pretending I am about to start from a clean focused state. The entry can be smaller because the day is being described honestly.

If the problem feels bigger than today and more like the whole week is pressing on you, PRISMA Week can help make that weight less solid. Not by turning life into a productivity system, but by helping you stop holding every day at once.

The smallest solution is often less self-translation

What helped me most was translating the stuckness less morally. Not immediately saying: I am doing nothing, so I am failing. More: I am stuck somewhere between wanting and starting. That is a different problem. And it needs a different kind of response.

As long as you read every hard day as a lack of effort, you will keep reaching for more force. But many of these days need less pressure, fewer choices and a smaller opening.
